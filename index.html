About

This campaign is built on a simple reality: one-year, in-person LL.M. students should leave TAMU Law with real relationships, real guidance, and real access — not just good intentions.

The goal is not to reinvent the school. The goal is to close the gaps that LL.M. students face: integration, bar readiness, interviewing and hiring, immigration clarity, and a clear path to use the Aggie Network. This platform is designed to deliver immediate wins and build a route that future representatives can continue.

Platform

Each pillar includes actions that can be delivered now and institutional seeds that build durable change.

1 Networking & LL.M.–J.D. Integration

LL.M. value is wasted when connections stay inside one classroom. Integration must be engineered.

What will be done ⌄

Deliver this term

  • “Aggie Introductions” mixers that pair LL.M. + J.D. students in short rotations.
  • LL.M.–J.D. mentorship matchmaking (opt-in) focused on class integration and professional introductions.
  • Global Expertise Registry: a simple directory of LL.M. jurisdictions and strengths for students and alumni.

Seed for continuity

  • Co-sponsored events between LL.M. community and existing student orgs (journals, advocacy teams, affinity groups).
  • Recurring calendar slots so integration events become institutional, not occasional.
2 Bar Readiness & Transparent Roadmap

The bar is hard enough. The bigger problem is uncertainty and lack of a tested LL.M. playbook.

What will be done ⌄

Deliver this term

  • Publish a “Texas Bar Roadmap” timeline: course planning, deadlines, and study milestones.
  • Peer-led bar accountability groups (weekly) to build discipline and reduce isolation.
  • Alumni playbooks: short, anonymized “what worked” bar prep strategies from recent LL.M. passers.

Seed for continuity

  • Formal proposal for an optional LL.M. bar-support track (pilot model; requires collaboration).
  • Annual data collection: survey-based bar outcomes for transparency and planning.
3 Professional Brand & Employer Access

LL.M. candidates must convert foreign training into a U.S.-market story: value, proof, and fit.

What will be done ⌄

Deliver this term

  • Resume and LinkedIn templates built for foreign-trained lawyers and U.S. screening logic.
  • Employer panel: firms and in-house counsel who actually hire international profiles.
  • “Global Advantage” messaging guide: how to sell bilingualism, cross-border expertise, and civil-law fluency.

Seed for continuity

  • Pilot an “International Talent Highlight” for the next recruitment cycle (with Career Services).
  • Create repeatable employer relationships tied to DFW, Houston, and Texas industry needs.
4 Interview Prep That Matches Reality

LL.M. interviews are different. Prep should reflect the actual questions international candidates face.

What will be done ⌄

Deliver this term

  • Mock interviews with alumni and practicing attorneys who have interviewed international candidates.
  • Question bank: “Tell me about your jurisdiction,” “Why U.S.?,” “Visa timeline,” “Writing sample expectations.”
  • Short workshops: how to answer with U.S. structure (STAR/IRAC), not home-country style.

Seed for continuity

  • Formal proposal for a recurring LL.M. mock-interview module within existing Career Services programming.
  • Build a rotating volunteer roster of alumni interviewers.
5 Visa Clarity (CPT/OPT and Beyond)

Confusion about work authorization creates paralysis. Clarity creates action and earlier planning.

What will be done ⌄

Deliver this term

  • Publish an LL.M. Immigration Guide (plain-English flowcharts for CPT/OPT timelines).
  • Host a Q&A session with an immigration attorney focused on law-student scenarios.
  • Create a “visa questions for employers” script so interviews become clean and confident.

Seed for continuity

  • Coordinate recurring sessions each semester with updated timelines and common pitfalls.
  • Build an FAQ archive so each incoming class starts ahead.
6 Activate the Aggie Network for Global Practice

The Aggie Network is a competitive advantage — but it must be operational, not symbolic.

What will be done ⌄

Deliver this term

  • Alumni “warm intro” nights (DFW + Houston) specifically for LL.M. career outcomes.
  • Volunteer alumni directory: practice areas + willingness to review resumes or do mock interviews.
  • Monthly “Global Practice Spotlight” featuring an alumnus using cross-border expertise.

Seed for continuity

  • Create an annual routine of alumni events and mentorship sign-ups for each incoming class.
  • Build partnerships with alumni chapters for sustained programming.
7 Marketable Focus in a General LL.M. (Without Pretending It’s a JD)

“General” must still look strategic. Employers hire for capability and signal — not just a degree title.

What will be done ⌄

Deliver this term

  • “Focus paths” that package course selection into employer-readable lanes (e.g., business/compliance, litigation, IP, energy).
  • Portfolio checklist: writing samples, short publications, and practical proof of skills (not just classes).
  • Introduce a simple “skills badge” concept that highlights concrete competencies (drafting, compliance, tax, contracts).

Seed for continuity

  • Formal proposal for faculty-backed micro-credentials or certificates aligned with Texas market demand.
  • Continue building employer pipelines that match those focus paths.
8 Equal Voice: Voting Rights for LL.M. Representatives in the SBA

Representation without voting power is not representation. LL.M. students should have a vote on procedural and substantive SBA matters.

What will be done ⌄

Deliver this term

  • Initiate a formal SBA task force to draft and propose an amendment granting LL.M. reps voting power on all SBA matters.
  • Publish a clear amendment roadmap: drafting, stakeholder support, vote process, and timeline.
  • Build coalition support across JD sections by showing mutual benefit: stronger global networking and employer reach.

Proof it’s feasible elsewhere

  • Harvard Law’s Student Government includes elected LL.M. representatives and gives each representative a vote on all substantive and procedural matters.
  • Peer programs demonstrate that LL.M. governance inclusion improves engagement and outcomes.
